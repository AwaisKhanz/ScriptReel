On September 5, 1977, NASA launched a spacecraft with an ambitious mission—to explore the outer planets of our solar system. It was called Voyager 1.

Originally, the mission was expected to last just a few years. But nearly five decades later, Voyager 1 is still traveling through space, making it the most distant human-made object ever created.

After flying past Jupiter in 1979, Voyager captured breathtaking images of its giant storms and discovered active volcanoes on Jupiter's moon, Io—the first volcanoes ever observed beyond Earth.

In 1980, the spacecraft continued to Saturn, revealing stunning details of its rings and providing humanity's first close-up views of the mysterious moon Titan.

Then, Voyager 1 did something extraordinary. Instead of stopping, it continued its journey toward the edge of the Solar System.

In 1990, before leaving the planetary neighborhood forever, NASA commanded Voyager to turn around one last time. From nearly six billion kilometers away, it captured a tiny image of Earth—a barely visible dot suspended in a beam of sunlight.

Astronomer Carl Sagan later called it "The Pale Blue Dot," reminding us that every person who has ever lived exists on that tiny speck.

In 2012, Voyager 1 became the first spacecraft in history to enter interstellar space, crossing beyond the protective bubble created by the Sun's solar wind.

Today, it is more than 25 billion kilometers from Earth. Its signals travel at the speed of light, yet they still take more than 23 hours to reach us.

On board is a golden record containing music, greetings in 55 languages, natural sounds from Earth, and images of humanity—a message intended for any intelligent civilization that might discover it millions of years from now.

Its power is slowly fading, and one day Voyager 1 will fall silent. But its journey will continue.

Long after Earth, the Sun, and even humanity are gone, Voyager 1 will keep drifting through the Milky Way, carrying the story of our civilization into the unknown.

A tiny spacecraft... A tiny blue planet... And humanity's greatest journey into the endless darkness of space.
